Letter from Karachi: Let students be their own evaluators!

Suraya Islam, fellow at Teach for Pakistan, thought of making students their own evaluators â€“ freeing them from the shackles of performance-related expectations and the fear of failure

Suraya Islam

Published on 10 October 2016

Environmental Science
7th - 8th
Impact Quotient (80%)

Three hours spent shuttling between the home and school. Every single day. As part of the trek, Suraya Islam would pass through rugged roads, mountains, crossing rivers and bylanes of Chitral, located in the northwest region of Pakistan. A decade later, Suraya sits talking to a group of girls at a government school in Karachi. Suraya is working as a fellow with the Teach for Pakistan – a profile that requires her to change her outlook towards conventional teaching methods.

It is not through any earth-shattering method that Suraya does it. She believes what she has is the most powerful weapon that every teacher can manage: Empathy.

“It used be a one-and a half hour journey to the school in Chitral. I was the only girl from my village to go to the school those days – jostling for space with the boys,” Suraya Islam, a young and bright teacher from Pakistan, recalls. Those arduous journeys have now brought Suraya close to the children she teachers every day. Today, teaching elementary schools girls at the Gizri School, Suraya thinks it is this reality that helps her empathise with the students. Her childhood in Chitral, a hilly district in the Khyber Pakhtunkhwa province of Pakistan, has been instrumental in shaping her perspective towards creative pedagogy.

Located in the heart of Karachi, the Gizri School has an attendance of up to 1200 students, taught by over 40 teachers. Students come from the poorest of the poor in Karachi to receive free education from Kindergarten all the way up to Matric (Secondary) qualification. Suraya teaches science and maths to young girls.

Suraya always knew the challenges, fears, apprehensions that young children come to school with, especially girls. She related her own experiences well with the way elementary school students found themselves vulnerable to the fear of judgments and performance. Their lives circled around the exam evaluations -- and this is what Suraya felt needs to change.

The dynamics of exam evaluations were not so easy to change. They were an instrumental part of the educational everyday life. The schools in Karachi are generally seen following a monthly test format, in which teachers teach for three weeks every month and then the students are tested on this content in the form of a monthly test in each subject. The results of these tests are added to the students’ report cards, and a cumulative result is prepared at the end of each academic year.

Suraya prepared a blueprint that had a central message: making students in-charge of their work. This didn’t mean that her role as a teacher had been diluted. Suraya now had to make sure that she keeps a watch over her students’ performance without making exams a ‘burdensome’ task. It was about a continuous and comprehensive academic evaluation – minus the fear of failure.

“I am a firm believer in data collection. My data helps me make sense of students’ performance and plan my lessons and after-school remedial classes accordingly. So Instead of tracking students’ progress myself, I made them in charge of monitoring their own work. Each student was given a tracker, which included all their unit tests, and was in the form of a bar graph. The students would colour their bar red or green, depending on whether they failed or passed a test. The visual representation of their scores helped capture these young students’ interest, and colouring in their graphs made them work harder, since everyone wanted to be able to color their bars green instead of red. Also, the more you scored, the longer your bar on the graph and the more you got to colour,” Suraya shares her experience.

With younger students, this worked really well because it was an interesting and enjoyable activity for them. The pressure of exams and evaluation vanished, Suraya noted. At the end of the month, Suraya would indulge in a stock taking mission – where the students would reflect on their performance. They would themselves be called to assess the probable areas of improvement and the different ways in which topics could be understood.

Suraya also took note of difficulties that were peculiar to the government school set-up: class strength, infrastructure and the challenging contexts that students came from.

“Teaching forty 9-year olds is no easy feat. I developed a classroom management plan in the beginning of the year, and only afterwards dealt with everything else,” Suraya says.

The classroom management plan involves class groups. Each group has a leader who has a score sheet, who writes down the points the teacher gives out. The points are awarded on good behaviour, following instructions, neat work, class participation, making an effort, etc.

For the age-group (7-13) that Suraya was involved with, this enhanced sharing, coordination and team-work through peer relations. “Yes, I had to make sure that this process of awarding points is always very positive,” Suraya points out.
