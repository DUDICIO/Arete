Math, memories and a mission called ‘Late Latif’

A teacher decided to twist the way mathematics was perceived. A tough task after all -- as not many embrace numbers with love. Here’s maths educator Anju Anand’s journey that had a small childhood memory leading to a mission called ‘late latif’.

Anju Anand

Published on 15 October 2016

Mathematics
7th - 8th
Impact Quotient (80%)

A fading memory from her own childhood ticked off something Anju Anand had never ever imagined! Every morning, when Anju lined up as a student for the morning assembly at Vidyaranya School, the principal Shantamma would ask: what is the most important thing in your life? Anju would glance at her wrist watch and tell herself: being on time.

Several years later, the tables have turned.

Anju, now a teacher in the same school, sees children coming late to the school. It irks her to think that punctuality has not really been a priority for children. Seeing this everyday did something magical to Anju’s mathematic classes with the fifth graders. Time made Anju change the way her students perceived mathematics. There began the journey of Operation Late Lateef.

“I love numbers. I feel numbers talk to me. It hurts me when I hear of children being afraid of mathematics. Math is all around us and it should not be enclosed in a textbook. For young children, understanding mathematics with abstract numbers can be challenging. Numbers have to come alive for them to really connect and relate to what they are being taught,” Anju says.

She had been closely observing how students would often get bored when themes like area, volume, density, and magnitude were taught using the textbook style. She was convinced that someway seemingly dead digits could be infused with life.

On a bright December morning, Anju gave a task to her fifth graders: collect data on students who come late to the school. She decided to bring punctuality and mathematics together in the mission called ‘Operation Late Latif’, a name given by one of her students.

She divided her students into five groups and asked each group to come up with a proposal. It was a mission the young ones were all excited to pull off! Each group began working out minute details: which group would be posted where in the morning? How they would count the number of people crossing by? How much time is wasted when students miss the punctuality mark? Each group etched out several questions that would be an asset for data collection at the end.

The idea was to start the counting mission right after the school bell rang at 7. a.m. Two groups were supposed to stand at the school gate, two in the parking lot, while others were responsible for keeping a track of the time. To make sure they recorded accurate data, the students realized they also had to know the number of children who were absent. This task was assigned to another group.

Voila! The plan of action was all set to unfold over the next twelve days!

There came twelve days of incessant excitement, running around and number trivia.It was a classroom bursting with palpable anxiety when the groups met their teacher. What would each data set add up to – and well, who had fetched what, they all thought!

For Anju, the mission had just begun! She had to bring mathematics in now. And why not? When it was the talk of ‘big data’ hovering around, it was high time the students learned to dismantle the fear that mathematics induced amongst them.

Using visual tools as an aid was one of the starting steps towards the mission. All the data that the students had collected was presented visually – and it gave the perfect opportunity to introduce the students to the concept of bar graphs. A bar was drawn for each day and a colour was used for representing the different sets of numbers collected. Initially it was hard to draw any definite conclusions from the data. The only thing that was clear was that more than half the children came late on the second day, and nearly two thirds were late on the fifth day, when it was raining very heavily in the morning.

In the midst came discussions on sample sizes and Anju explained to the students the importance of the right size and how small samples could skew data. More maths meant more magic in the classroom.

To make more sense of the data, Anju decided the class should take into account the average numbers. A pie chart was drawn using the average that was calculated by the students. This was the first time the children were actually beginning to understand the concept of average, which was really strange because they come across that idea so often in their daily lives – like average run rates or average rates of goals.

Be it the football field or their mother’s grocery tasks, the children began connecting the mundane to maths. “I will not say that I saw a big change in children’s behaviour after this project, but awareness was definitely higher. My class did try to be on time and felt proud of themselves when they made it to school before the bell. This is just a beginning,” feels Anju.

Creative Curator
Anju Anand

Anju Anand is an engineer by training. However, after a series of stints in the corporate world, she found her calling in teaching. She began her journey of teaching with “Khel Khel Mein”, an experimental mathematics lab. Now, a teacher of mathematics and geography in middle school, Anju believes in taking a practical approach to teaching concepts that are often quite abstract for a child.
