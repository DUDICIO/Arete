Every child is different

A classroom is a world full of possibilities, strengths and limitations. Tulika Bathija shares how literature can be an effective medium to shape an atmosphere of joyful learning.

Tulika Bathija

Published on 13 October 2016

Literature
English
7th - 8th
Impact Quotient (80%)

As an educator, I believe it my responsibility to acknowledge that every child is unique, exhibiting a range of personality traits, strengths and limitations. Often as teachers, we cater to their limitations alone and ignore their strengths. When we tap their strengths into our daily lessons, the classroom environment is more engaging and healthy.

In my grade 7 class, there are 20 students. Currently, there are three students with learning needs. One of them has very low self-esteem and has been diagnosed with mild dyslexia and dysgraphia. He struggles to organize his thoughts, dislikes reading and fails to incorporate new vocabulary in his writing. He is well aware of his limitations but what heâ€™s not aware of is that he has a sharp memory.

This was then presented in a tabular format.

Students used this table to write the first draft of their essays. For students like Rishi (name changed) & many others who struggle with organization, it helps if they have a written framework, so they can focus on presenting their ideas and writing them fluently. It is important to assess what students know and not belittle them.

#Teachertip: Create tasks that will motivate students and test what they know or what they have learnt. It is absolutely acceptable to refer to books, planning sheets and not give your own opinion and ideas. Treating them as mature, thinking individuals and offering necessary support will not just enhance the quality of learning but also help build rapport.
