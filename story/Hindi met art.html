When poetry and Hindi met art in a classroom!

Teaching poetry in a language class is an unassuming challenge. But Nirmal tells us how visuals and imaginative communication can be a game changer when it comes to teaching Hindi poetry!

Nirmal Kumari

Published on 12 October 2016

Hindi
Literature
7th - 8th
Impact Quotient (80%)

It is close to 11 a.m. as Kavita and her friends return to the classroom after the school lunch break. The girls have their class 11 Hindi textbook ‘Antra’ neatly placed on the tables. They sift through pages – and stop at page number 139. While they wait for the Hindi teacher to arrive, there is a palpable excitement that the young students find hard to conceal. 

With eyes set on the NCERT book, the class full of 40 girls doodle on their notebooks – a sight surprising enough ahead of a Hindi class. However, it is not an unusual sight for Nirmal Kumari, the Hindi teacher who will take a poetry class with the vibrant group. Nirmal quickly picks a few colourful chalks and turns towards the blackboard to draw. The sun, trees, waterfall -- together merging into a scenic image as the girls watched with rapt attention.

The three-page long poem ‘संध्या के बाद’ (what follows the dusk) by noted Hindi writer Sumitranandan Pant had never been so interesting for the students until she thought of using visual storytelling to teach poetry. Declining interest in Hindi literature pushed Nirmal to conceptualise a novel and creative way to take poetry to her students. Nirmal named the exercise ‘आओ कविता पढ़ें’ and mostly devoted 30 minutes for visual mapping of the poem before the class.

Across three 40-minute periods in a week, the poem flows through visuals drawn by the teacher. Students contribute and interact as they understand sentences through visuals and associated word-meanings.

Amongst a group that had grown in ‘concretised’ urban environments – and most of them had only heard of rivers, mountains and waterfalls in books. Visuals in आओ कविता पढ़ें took the students right where the action was happening in the poem. It brought them closer to the world that the poem was located in. Infact, the practice also permeated into other themes such as Hindi stories, biographical lessons and poems on social issues.  

There was a visible change in the learning environment, Nirmal noticed. The students come from low-income groups where some students even work after the school to earn a living. In such challenging contexts where the students cannot find a suitable environment to study after school, it is the classroom where teachers can create a new world for children.

“And there can be no better medium than language”  

Language was made a creative venture for the students, who otherwise relied on textbooks or verbal lectures. With two-decade long teaching experience behind her, Nirmal also realised that children have a short attention span. For a poem, and even lessons that were long, visuals and creative means of expressions were what kept the students abound.

“My primary method has been good storytelling because you cannot teach language to children unless you connect it to their everyday lives,” feels Nirmal.

But for a language that is often “taken lightly” by not just students, but even teachers, Nirmal’s challenge was more of the mind, than the method. It was about rekindling the spark for Hindi all over again.

“In a classroom with over 40 students, each student has a different level of understanding. They perceive differently, respond differently and have diverse learning needs. But children love to learn only when they feel emotionally involved,” the 56-year old teacher says.   

It is important to keep the elements of poetry and literary essence alive while teaching language – be it English or Hindi. As teachers we have to also keep in mind that books change according to political parties. And this has a direct impact on our students because what they are going to study is being constantly manipulated and reshaped.

At the same time, their needs as learners are very diverse. As teachers we engage with young girls who come from poor families. They do not have a supportive environment to study at home. So in this case classroom is the space where we, as teachers, can make a difference for them. This is where we can make education interesting and viable for them.
