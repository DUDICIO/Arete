Fun with Latitudes and Longitudes

Teaching Longitudes and Lattitudes is always a challenge for teachers. What do the degrees indicate? Are latitudes and longitudes relevant to us? Why are the north pole and south pole ﬁxed points? These are some questions that my students ask. Here is how to make them learn that.

Smita Uchil

Published on 14 October 2016

Geography
4th - 6th
Impact Quotient (80%)

Most students rarely realize that the degrees we use for measuring latitudes and longitudes are the same as the ones we use in geometry. Many children use GPS when they are travelling but not all of them know that latitudes and longitudes are used by the system to trace locations. A lot of children also believe that the north pole and south pole are fi xed points because the globe is held by a stand.

How I taught latitudes and longitudes

I took the children to the ground and drew a 10x10 grid. I numbered the 10 horizontal lines 1 to 10 and the 10 vertical lines A to J. Then I gave each child a chit that told him/her where he/she should stand in the grid – 1B, 2D, etc. I set a time limit in which the children should reach their positions. One child was asked to catch those children who weren’t in their position on time.

Later I divided the class into groups of fi ve and gave each group a softball. Two of these softballs had latitudes and longitudes drawn on them and the rest did not have any markings. The children had to use a marker to plot a point on the softball and then identify the location of the point. How tricky this small task became for the groups that didn’t have any latitudes and longitudes marked! This task drove home the need for latitudes and longitudes. Then a knitting needle was inserted into the ball to represent the axis so now we had two fi xed points which we named, north pole and south pole.

Finding a middle point on the axis we divided the softballs into two halves. Now we had two hemispheres. The one on the top was called the northern hemisphere and the one at the bottom was called the southern hemisphere and the line at which we had cut the ball was the equator.

For the sake of convenience we cut the northern hemisphere vertically into two halves. We placed the 180 degree protractor at the centre of the diameter of the Earth (softball) and measured 23½º on both sides and then joined the two points to get the semicircle of the measure 23½º. Similarly we measured 66½º.

Now we placed a 360-degree protractor on the southern hemisphere and measured several longitudes and drew arcs joining these degrees to the south pole thus getting the longitudes. Since we were doing this on a 3D fi gure, it helped correct many misconceptions that the children had had.

“Geography is all about making connections! It’s a wonderful interconnection between science, mathematics and earth sciences.”

This was a comment by our former principal Mr. Sundaram. This inspired me to try and understand the topic in greater detail and take up the challenge of getting the children to do things instead of just getting them to draw latitudes and longitudes on the board.
