Breaking borders through literature

Tulika Bathija tells us how literature can be used to celebrate difference and cherish diversity in a classroom.

Tulika Bathija

Published on 17 October 2016

Literature
English
6yh - 8th
Impact Quotient (80%)

When the Head of the English department shared with me that one of the units in the Language and Literature IB MYP (Middle Years Programme) curriculum for grade 6 students is Hope, a peace building initiative was the first thing that struck me. I discussed the idea with my HOD and the grade 6 teacher who taught the other class. We all agreed to weave in the theme of peace and friendship into this unit.

As part of our unit, we studied the text, Bridge to Terabithia by Katherine Paterson. Now, of course in terms of context and textual-themes, it is in no way related to friendship between India and Pakistan. But, it is a book that celebrates difference, cherishes diversity and gives us hope that humanity can co-exist in peace and harmony despite differences. No matter how different two people are, we can connect and build stronger bonds of friendship.

Thought for Teachers

  1. When you introduce a class text, you often have no control over the selection, but you can play around with the story and allow students to take from it something more meaningful than just the basic elements of a story.
  2. Think of the topics and themes that the story deals with – power, friendship, violence, peace, morality etc.
  3. Think of global issues and recent events you can connect your themes to –If your book is about the Second World War, think of civil war in the present times like the Syrian conflict.
  4. Find out how you can make a difference as students and educators. Can you do a poster campaign? Write letters or start drives or movements?
  5. As English teachers, we are hard-pressed to cover language units like persuasive writing or letter writing. Find ways you can address textual themes through writing skills and use current global situations as content.
  6. This process helps students understand that literature is nothing but life itself.

 

Unit Plan for peace-building initiatives

Aim: To write letters to students in conflict-torn regions – Kashmir, North East (Shillong) & Pakistan (Islamabad)

Writing skill: Letter writing

Theme: Friendship

Activity

  1. Indian students researched and find out information about Kashmir, Shillong & Pakistan in groups.
  2. Information related to food, cultural traditions, politics, languages and people was presented in groups through charts and posters.
  3. The above activity was to get a basic understanding of these regions through media.
  4. Students prepared a list of questions about what they would like to ask their Pakistani, North-East or Kashmiri pen friends.

E.g. What clothes do you usually wear?

How is the education system?

What books do you like to read?

  1. Students were encouraged to ask personal and non-political questions so that they could connect with each other at a personal level to find out how similar or different they truly are.
  2. Students wrote letters including their questions and share personal information about themselves.
  3. The letters were posted to the respective schools in Kashmir, Islamabad and Shillong.
  4. Students in those schools read and responded to letters sent from Bombay.
  5. Once their letters reached Bombay, students read them and found out firsthand how similar their habits & interests are.

Any form of writing activity needs a purpose and an audience. Students will be interested in writing if they are asked to write to “real” people. Making “real” friends and building bridges was the main purpose. But, through this activity students learnt how to write informal letters as well. As teachers, even with a restricted, rigid curriculum we can explore the texts and themes to innovate and create new teaching strategies and enriching learning experiences.

Student a: "This was an amazing exercise. I learnt a lot about how the children in Kashmir are just like us. This exercise also shattered our myths … what we assume on the basis of what we see in news. All of them aren't poor but some are actually rich if I may say. I was also really impressed with the handwriting."

Student b: The activity that we did was wonderful. I had the opportunity to connect to the amazing students of Shillong. I found it very meaningful to communicate in the form of letters. I would love to thank Ms. Tulika for this. This activity was much different from the others because when we write no one really sees or views it. But this time we did get a letter back!"

Student c: “I was very excited when I first heard about this activity. I sent my letter to a student in Pakistan. I was very nervous when I got the letter. This was a very unique activity because when a person thinks about English they think essays or story writing!"

It also brought us closer to the art of letter writing … with time we are switching to faster means of communication and

Student d: "I loved writing and receiving those letters to and from the children who lived in Pakistan. The letter that I got was beautiful and uplifting, I had never thought that a person that I had never met could be so kind or sweet it was fantastic. When I gave them my letter I made sure to be as caring and sweet as she was and I think I did that. Thank you to Ms. Tulika for conducting this activity."

Student e: "Through this activity I learnt that not everybody is the same but each one of us will have a similar point. This was a unit initiated activity and the letter I received was in such good handwriting that I thought it was printed. The letter was short but I loved the language he used. I wrote to a student in Pakistan. This activity was very touching and helpful."

Tulika Bathija is an ESL specialist and English teacher at Ecole Mondiale World School, Mumbai. She has done extensive research in children's narrative writing. She hold a BA in English Literature and has completed her MA and MPhil in English Language Education from The English and Foreign Languages University, Hyderabad. She is in search of her Sindhi heritage and ardently participates in Indo-Pak peace initiatives.
